How to Write Dialogue

Dialogue is a conversation between two or more characters in a story, play, or script. Well-written dialogue advances the plot, reveals character traits, and creates an engaging reading experience.

Basic Structure

  1. Format: Always begin a new paragraph when switching speakers.
  2. Punctuation: Use quotation marks around spoken words and proper punctuation inside them.
  3. Attribution: Include dialogue tags (e.g., "said," "asked") to identify speakers.
  4. Action: Integrate action beats to show what characters are doing during conversations.
  5. Purpose: Ensure each dialogue exchange serves a purpose in your story.

Do's

  • Write dialogue that sounds natural but is more focused than real conversation
  • Use contractions and incomplete sentences when appropriate
  • Include character-specific speech patterns
  • Balance dialogue with narration and action
  • Read dialogue aloud to test how it sounds

Don'ts

  • Overuse fancy dialogue tags instead of "said" and "asked"
  • Include excessive greetings and small talk
  • Use dialogue as an information dump
  • Write all characters with the same speech pattern
  • Forget punctuation rules for dialogue

Dialogue Examples

Example 1: Basic Dialogue

Ram: "Have you finished your homework yet?"
Sita: "Not yet. I'm still working on the math problems."
Ram: "Do you need any help? I finished mine already."
Sita: "That would be great! These equations are really difficult."

This example shows a simple conversation with clear attribution of who is speaking. Note how each speaker starts on a new line.

Example 2: Dialogue with Action

Teacher: "Is everyone ready for the test?"
The teacher looked around the classroom, noting several worried faces.
Hari: "I studied all night, but I'm still nervous."
He tapped his pencil anxiously against his desk.
Teacher: "Just do your best. Remember what we practiced."
She smiled reassuringly as she handed out the test papers.

This example incorporates action beats that show what characters are doing while speaking, adding context and emotional depth.

Example 3: Dialogue in a Story Context

The market was crowded with shoppers on that sunny Saturday morning. Priya weaved through the throng, searching for her friend who was supposed to meet her by the fruit stall.

Priya: "Anita! Over here!"
She waved her arm above the crowd.
Anita: "Sorry I'm late. The bus was delayed again."
Anita hurried over, adjusting her bag on her shoulder.
Priya: "No problem. I just got here myself. Should we start with the vegetable section?"
Anita: "Actually, can we get some tea first? I'm exhausted from running to catch that bus."

Priya nodded and the two friends headed toward the small tea stall at the edge of the market, the aroma of fresh spices and flowers following them as they went.

This example shows dialogue integrated within narrative context, with a clear setting and situation that frames the conversation.

Tips for Effective Dialogue

Character Voice

Each character should have a unique way of speaking that reflects their:

  • Background: Education level, regional dialect, cultural influences
  • Personality: Formal or casual, verbose or terse, optimistic or pessimistic
  • Relationship: How they speak to different people (friends vs. authority figures)

Example: A professor might say, "I'm afraid I cannot attend the function due to prior commitments," while a teenager might say, "Can't make it. Got stuff to do."

Dialogue Tags

Dialogue tags attribute speech to specific characters. Keep these guidelines in mind:

  • Use "said" and "asked" most often as they're nearly invisible to readers
  • Use more specific tags (whispered, shouted, muttered) sparingly and only when necessary
  • Consider replacing dialogue tags with action beats when appropriate

Example with tags: "I'm not sure about this," she said. "We should be careful," he warned.

Example with action: "I'm not sure about this." She bit her lip. "We should be careful." He glanced over his shoulder.

Subtext

Subtext is what characters really mean but don't say explicitly. It adds depth to dialogue by:

  • Creating tension between what's said and what's meant
  • Revealing character motivations and feelings
  • Engaging readers who must interpret the underlying meaning
Meera: "So you're going to Raj's party tonight?"
Arun: "I might stop by for a while."
Meera: "I see. Well, have fun."
She turned away quickly, pretending to organize her books.

The subtext here might be that Meera is disappointed Arun is going to the party, perhaps because she wasn't invited or because she has feelings for him.
