News Article Structure

A news article follows a specific structure designed to deliver information clearly and efficiently. The most important information comes first (the inverted pyramid structure).

1. Headline

A concise, attention-grabbing statement that summarizes the main point of the article. Should be accurate and not misleading.

Example: "Local School Wins National Science Competition"

2. Byline

The name of the reporter who wrote the article and sometimes the date of publication.

Example: "By Rajendra Sharma | April 15, 2023"

3. Lead (First Paragraph)

The opening paragraph that answers the 5W1H questions (Who, What, When, Where, Why, and How) about the most important aspects of the story.

Example: "A team of students from Bright Future Academy in Kathmandu won first place in the National Science Innovation Challenge on Saturday, beating 50 other schools with their solar-powered water purification system."

4. Body

Subsequent paragraphs that provide additional details, background information, quotes from relevant sources, and context. Information is arranged in descending order of importance.

5. Quotes

Direct statements from people involved in or affected by the news event. Adds credibility and human interest to the story.

Example: ""We worked on this project for six months," said team leader Priya Thapa, 16. "It's designed to help rural communities access clean drinking water.""

6. Additional Information

Context, background, or related information that helps readers understand the significance of the news.

News Writing Guidelines

Writing a news article requires adherence to specific principles to ensure accuracy, clarity, and objectivity.

Do's

  • Be accurate and factual
  • Write in clear, simple language
  • Use active voice and present tense for recent events
  • Include reliable sources and quotes
  • Maintain objectivity and balance
  • Use short paragraphs (2-3 sentences)
  • Focus on the most important information first

Don'ts

  • Include personal opinions or bias
  • Use flowery or unnecessary language
  • Write long, complex sentences
  • Include speculation or unverified information
  • Bury important information in the middle or end
  • Use jargon without explanation
  • Make claims without attribution

The 5W1H Formula

Every good news article should answer these questions in the first paragraph:

  • Who: Who is involved in the news event?
  • What: What happened?
  • When: When did it happen?
  • Where: Where did it happen?
  • Why: Why did it happen?
  • How: How did it happen?

Example News Article

LOCAL STUDENTS DEVELOP APP TO REDUCE FOOD WASTE
By Manisha Patel | May 10, 2023

A group of six students from Kathmandu Model College has developed a mobile application that connects restaurants and grocery stores with local charities to reduce food waste in the city.

The app, named "FoodShare," allows businesses to post information about surplus food that would otherwise be thrown away. Local organizations can then claim these donations and arrange pickup times.

"We were shocked to learn that approximately one-third of all food produced in Nepal goes to waste, while many people don't have enough to eat," said Aditya Sharma, the 19-year-old team leader and computer science student. "Our app creates a simple solution to connect excess food with those who need it most."

Since its launch three weeks ago, FoodShare has facilitated the donation of over 500 kg of food that would have otherwise been discarded. Fifteen restaurants and five grocery stores in Kathmandu have signed up for the service.

Maya Restaurant in Thamel was one of the first businesses to join the platform. "We often have leftover food at the end of the day that's still perfectly good," said owner Ramesh Gurung. "Before FoodShare, we had no efficient way to donate it. Now, it takes just seconds to post what we have available."

Sunita Rai, director of Hamro Samaj, a nonprofit organization that feeds homeless individuals, praised the initiative. "The app has streamlined our operations significantly. We know exactly what's available and when we can collect it, which helps us plan our meal services better," she said.

The student developers won 200,000 NPR in seed funding from a local tech competition last month, which they used to launch and market the app. They plan to expand to Pokhara and other major cities in Nepal within the next six months.

According to the Food and Agriculture Organization, food waste is a global problem with significant environmental impacts. When food decomposes in landfills, it produces methane, a greenhouse gas more potent than carbon dioxide.

The FoodShare app is available for free download on both Android and iOS platforms.

Analysis of the Example

  • Headline: Clear, concise, and captures the main point
  • Lead Paragraph: Answers who (students), what (developed an app), why (to reduce food waste), where (in Kathmandu)
  • Structure: Most important information first, followed by details, quotes, and context
  • Quotes: Includes perspectives from the developers, a participating business, and a beneficiary
  • Context: Provides background information about the food waste problem
  • Conclusion: Ends with practical information for readers who might want to use the app
